RDM Partners · A Thesis

Severity Is
the Moat

Why the most-feared corner of California healthcare is the one place a billion-dollar roll-up cannot follow, and the one price the state cannot simply set.

Palm Desert & the Central Valley · June 2026
§
"Invert, always invert."Charlie Munger

On a quiet street in California's Central Valley there is a six-bedroom house that looks like every other house on the block. There is a lawn. There is a basketball hoop bolted over the garage. Inside lives a man in his forties who, a generation ago, would have spent his life behind the walls of a state institution. He can no longer live with his parents; they cannot lift him, and they will not always be alive. He has behaviors that frighten landlords and a history that frightens almost everyone else. Roughly ninety-five cents of every dollar that keeps his house running, that pays the people who care for him in shifts around the clock, comes from a single government payor.

Every spreadsheet in private equity has been trained to recoil from that last sentence. One payor. Government rates. A population the press treats as a scandal waiting to be written. I have spent the better part of a year inside this corner of American healthcare, and I have come to believe close to the opposite of what the spreadsheets believe. The thing the market fears about this house is the same thing that makes it one of the most defensible cash flows I have ever underwritten.11This essay is written in the first person from the perspective of a control investor evaluating the lower-middle-market of California developmental-services providers. It refers throughout to "a Central Valley operator" rather than to any named company; the operator quotations are real and are drawn from a founder interview. Industry facts, statutes, and figures are public and cited. The fear is the moat.

Let me show you what I see.

I

The Consensus, and Why It Is Correct

"The private equity market just went bonkers over autism. We never, ever, ever, ever have seen that."Dexter Braff, healthcare M&A advisor

I want to begin by conceding the bear case in full, because it is not stupid. It is, in fact, correct, for the buyers it describes.

In 2018, Blackstone bought a majority stake in the Center for Autism and Related Disorders, the largest autism-services chain in the country, in a deal that valued it at roughly six hundred million dollars, more than twenty times its earnings.22NBC News investigation into CARD and Blackstone; proto.life (reporting CEPR's analysis); Behavioral Health Business on the July 2023 bankruptcy sale. Blackstone acquired roughly a 70% stake at a valuation near $600M; the Chapter 11 sale closed near $48.5M. The demand behind that business was, for every practical purpose, infinite: diagnoses climbing, waitlists stretching for years, a line out the door of every clinic. Five years later, in the summer of 2023, the company filed for bankruptcy and was sold out of Chapter 11 for about forty-eight million dollars. More than ninety percent of the equity value had evaporated against a rising tide of demand.

What killed it was not a shortage of customers. It was the customer's price. As the company's Medicaid share of revenue climbed from roughly a tenth in 2017 to better than a third by 2022, the book turned unprofitable, because the government paid less than the care cost to deliver. The company began discouraging the lower-billing children and chasing the higher-billing ones; it abandoned ten states, citing, in its own filings, "unsustainable reimbursement rates." A former executive put the lesson as plainly as it can be put: "In the end, we were upside down on our Medicaid population."33proto.life (Feb 2024), summarizing the Center for Economic and Policy Research. CARD's Medicaid share rose from about 10% (2017) to 37% (May 2022); the company lost roughly $82M in the year to April 2023 and carried about $245M of debt at filing. Demand was never the problem. Demand is not revenue when a government sets the price.

This is a pattern, not an unlucky deal. Sevita, the largest operator of disability group homes in the country, carries a junk credit rating after its owners pulled more than seven hundred million dollars in debt-funded dividends out of it and a sister company.44Moody's rated Sevita Caa1. Private Equity Stakeholder Project and Stateline (May 2025) document more than $737M in debt-funded dividends extracted by Sevita's and Help at Home's owners. When Alabama declined to raise a home-care rate that was already the lowest of any reporting state, the owner of Help at Home simply walked, ending forty-five years of operation and laying off hundreds of caregivers; a year later it paid itself a quarter-billion-dollar dividend. AdvoServ, which ran sixty-two group homes in New Jersey, ceased to exist entirely in 2019 after two residents choked to death and the state moved to pull its license. The advocacy world keeps a tidy summary of the arithmetic: a firm that has to double or triple its money in four to seven years, the Private Equity Stakeholder Project observes, finds that "challenging to do in IDD services without cutting costs in a way that compromises the quality of care."

So the consensus is not wrong. A leveraged national platform that buys this work, loads it with debt, and runs the standard cost-out playbook will, often enough, destroy both the asset and the people inside it. If that were the only way to own this cash flow, I would pass too. The argument of everything that follows is that it is not the only way, and that the very facts that doom the cost-cutter are the facts that protect the operator who does the opposite.

II

Demand Is Not Revenue

"Unjustified isolation is properly regarded as discrimination based on disability."Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S. 581 (1999)

Start with the demand, because it is the part everyone gets right.

The supply of family caregiving is collapsing on a demographic schedule you can set a watch by. Nearly a million American households contain an adult with an intellectual or developmental disability living with, and wholly dependent on, a caregiver who is themselves aging out of the ability to provide it.55Medicaid.gov, on supporting adults with IDD and their aging caregivers. CDC autism prevalence: about 1 in 36 children, up from roughly 1 in 150 in 2000. KFF: more than 710,000 people on Medicaid home- and community-based waiver waiting lists in 2024. Autism prevalence has gone from one child in a hundred fifty at the turn of the century to one in thirty-six today, and each of those diagnoses is a multi-decade service need walking slowly toward adulthood. Across the country, more than seven hundred thousand people sit on waiting lists for home- and community-based disability services, many for a decade. California alone serves around half a million people and adds roughly forty thousand a year, the equivalent, the state's own analysts note, of standing up an entire new regional center every twelve months. None of this is cyclical. People with severe disabilities need care in a recession and in a boom. The need is demographic and legal, not economic.

And yet every sentence in that paragraph describes demand, and demand is not revenue. The bankruptcy in the last section happened to a company with a hurricane of demand at its back. The throttle on this business was never the number of people who need care. The throttle is two things the public never sees: whether the state authorizes the placement, and what the state agrees to pay for it. To understand why some of these cash flows are fragile and some are nearly sovereign, you have to stop looking at the demand and start following the money.

The person is an entitlement. The provider is mostly a price-taker. And then there is the exception.

III

Severity Is the Moat

"There is no rate. The rate is the home."The founder of a Central Valley platform

Here is the exception. For the great mass of ordinary residential and day services, California has spent the last few years doing exactly what a cost-conscious payor does: it built a single statewide fee schedule, a rate model, and it is now squeezing it. As of this writing the state is ending, four months early, the protection that let providers keep their old above-model rates; after the end of February it pays the model and nothing more.66California Legislative Analyst's Office, 2025-26 Spending Plan; DDS budget update. The rate-reform "hold harmless" ends Feb 28, 2026 (a $75M General Fund saving), cutting above-model providers down to the model. The 10% quality bonus that rides on every rate is separately being tightened for about $221.7M in ongoing savings. If your home bills at the schedule, you are a price-taker, and the price is being cut.

But the schedule stops where severity begins. For the highest-acuity placements, the homes built for the most dangerous behaviors and the most complex needs, California does not read a rate off a chart. State law requires that the rate be assembled from the bottom up, one person at a time: a fixed amount for the house, and on top of it an individualized amount built from that specific resident's plan of care and the staffing their acuity demands, submitted to the regional center, justified, and approved.77California Welfare & Institutions Code §4684.81 (enhanced behavioral supports homes) and §4698 (community crisis homes); Title 17 CCR §59072 and §59022. Each sets a fixed facility component plus an individualized services-and-supports component developed per resident from the individual program plan, subject to regional-center review and written approval. The operator I have come to know describes it the way operators describe things, bluntly and a little too confidently: "There is no rate. The rate is the home." He is being rhetorical. There is a rate. It is simply not a number the state can ratchet down across a fee schedule, because there is no schedule here; there is a person, and a level of need, and a price that has to cover the cost of meeting it.

I want to be precise, because precision is the whole point. This is not a magic exemption. These placements still sit inside the state's rate architecture; the quality bonuses that ride on top are being tightened, and the auditors can and do claw back dollars that are not documented to the service code. Severity escapes the standardized rollback. It does not escape gravity. But here is what gives the individualized price its floor: the cost of the alternative. The only locked state institution California has left runs about a million dollars per resident per year, on the state's own dime, with no federal match at all. A community placement for the same person, even at what looks like a premium, costs the state far less, and draws a federal match besides.88Disability Rights California (Porterville Q&A); California LAO Report 3735 on developmental-center closure savings. A developmental-center bed historically ran $500K to $700K a year and Porterville's secure unit about $1M, entirely state-funded; a community placement runs a fraction of that and pulls roughly a 50% federal match. The state pays up for severity not out of generosity but because every other option is more expensive, and because the law has made the institution unavailable. When you sell the one thing the buyer cannot get anywhere cheaper, you are not a price-taker. You are negotiating against the cost of a jail cell and a state-hospital bed.

That is the rate side. Now the operating side, which is where the national platforms actually break.

There is a barbell in this industry. At one end is the low-acuity, high-volume, standardizable work: the day programs, the lighter group homes, the therapy hours that can be turned into a billing machine and rolled up across state lines. Private equity owns that end and is consolidating it aggressively, and good for them; it is a real business. At the other end is the severe work, and the severe work cannot be proceduralized. It is sixteen-hour shifts and twenty-four-hour shifts and a single staffing failure that becomes a death and then becomes a revoked license. The platforms break here, reliably, and the public record is not ambiguous about it. Sevita, the largest operator in the country, ran the highest-acuity home type California licenses. By the middle of 2023 it had surrendered its California enhanced behavioral home and exited that line in the state entirely, under regulator pressure, with whistleblowers describing staff worked around the clock.99KQED and the USC Center for Health Journalism (2023). California's Department of Developmental Services confirmed Sevita "no longer operates any EBSHs in California" after it relinquished its one enhanced home (the "Illinois Home") on June 30, 2023; the 16- and 24-hour shifts come from whistleblower accounts, not a published regulatory finding. The national champion was pushed out of precisely the service the severe end is made of.

You do not even have to read the failures to see it. You can read the winner. The most successful private-equity platform in this entire space, a pharmacy and home-health roll-up that went public and tripled, did not buy its way deeper into severe disability care. It sold its disability-residential business to the junk-rated buyer and walked toward the standardizable end. The smart money's revealed preference is to shed the severe end, not to own it. And the price tags follow the capital: scaled therapy platforms trade at twelve to fifteen times earnings, scaled disability residential around ten, and the small, single-region, high-acuity California operator at six to nine.1010RDM analysis of disclosed IDD and ABA transactions, 2024 to 2026. The pattern is consistent: the more severe and the more sub-scale the asset, the thinner the buyer field and the lower the multiple. The moat is widest exactly where the capital is thinnest. That is not a coincidence; it is the same fact seen from two sides. The work that scares the roll-up away is the work with the deepest barrier around it. A clean compliance record in this business cannot be bought, only earned, over years, and it can be destroyed in a night. A relationship with a regional center is measured in decades. A census never churns, because for these families leaving is a trauma, not a transaction. Severity is the moat.

IV

Where the People Go

"the only state hospital set up to treat criminal defendants with intellectual and developmental disabilities"CalMatters, on the last California institution

To understand why the state negotiates the price up rather than down, you have to ask the question the spreadsheets never ask. If this house did not exist, where would the man in it go?

A generation ago the answer was an institution. California ran a system of large state developmental centers, thirteen thousand beds at its peak. Over twenty-seven years the state closed almost all of it: Stockton, Camarillo, Agnews, Lanterman, Sonoma, Fairview, one after another, the last general-treatment unit shuttered in 2021.1111California DDS developmental-center closures and state facility records. The system was depopulated under the 1993 Coffelt settlement, which suspended new institutional admissions, and reinforced by Olmstead. Only Porterville's secure treatment program remains. What remains is a single locked facility, Porterville, and it is forensic only: you get in through the criminal courts, not through need. The blunt and consequential fact is that California no longer has a long-term, non-forensic state bed to fall back on. The route to a state institution now runs through an arrest.

So the man goes to the places that absorb people no one will house. He goes to jail, where in California people with cognitive disabilities make up as much as a third of the population, and where a man can wait, as one did, nearly nine years for a competency bed the state cannot supply.1212CalMatters (Dec 2022); the litigation in Stiavetti v. Clendenin over unconstitutional jail delays for defendants found incompetent to stand trial. Roughly 4,000 Californians a year are declared incompetent and warehoused in county jails awaiting a placement. He goes to a locked psychiatric ward, or he boards for weeks in an emergency room because there is nowhere to discharge him, or he goes home to parents no longer strong enough to keep him safe, until the day they cannot, and the crisis becomes the state's anyway, at a far higher price. The community capacity meant to catch him is nearly theoretical: for a system serving half a million people, California has on the order of twenty-five crisis homes and ninety-five enhanced behavioral homes, four beds each. That is the entire severe-end safety net.

This is the moral center of the thing, and it is also, not by accident, the source of the operator's pricing power. The state is not a generous buyer. It is a cornered one. It is forbidden by a thirty-year-old court settlement from reopening the institutions, and forbidden by the Americans with Disabilities Act, as the Supreme Court read it in Olmstead, from warehousing people it could serve in the community. It has a person it must place, a legal obligation it cannot escape, and almost no one willing to take the hardest cases. When I underwrite the severe end, I am not betting on the kindness of a government program. I am pricing the cost of the alternatives the government has spent thirty years making unavailable to itself.

V

What the State, and Washington, Want

"What is the goal of every bureaucracy? The welfare department is not trying to get smaller."The founder

If the state is a cornered buyer, it is worth understanding exactly what corners it, because the answer is not what an outsider would guess. The system is not driven by thrift. It is driven by two things: a legal obligation it cannot shed, and an appetite to grow.

California is the only state in the country that treats disability services as a true entitlement. Under a 1969 law, anyone the system finds eligible has a right to services, with no waiting list and no cap, and in 1985 the state Supreme Court held that the government may not quietly cut those services to balance a budget.1313The Lanterman Developmental Disabilities Services Act (1969); Association for Retarded Citizens v. DDS, 38 Cal.3d 384 (1985), holding the state may not administratively reduce statutory services for budget reasons. The Legislative Analyst's Office describes the resulting budget as "non-discretionary" and caseload-driven. That single ruling is why, in every downturn, the state reaches for the one lever it is allowed to pull. It cannot turn people away, so it squeezes the price. It froze provider rates in 2003 and held them frozen for a decade; it cut them again through the Great Recession; it is squeezing them now, ending the rate protection early and tightening the quality bonus, while an organized lobby of parents and providers fights it to a draw every June.1414LAO 2012-13 Developmental Services Budget Update: rates frozen from 2003, a 3% provider reduction (FY2009-10) raised to a cumulative 4.25% (FY2010-11 through FY2011-12), lifted July 1, 2013. After a decade of suppression, the 2019 Burns rate study found about $1.8B of underfunding, and 2024 rate reform raised many rates 30 to 50%. Rate risk runs in both directions. The politician's revealed preference is exact: protect the count of people served, loudly, and squeeze the price paid to providers, quietly.

But the deeper driver is growth, and here the operator's cynicism is more clarifying than any policy paper. The state does not experience a new disabled Californian as a cost. It experiences him as a draw on the federal treasury. Every person it can move onto a federally matched waiver brings Washington's money into California, which is how a program that cost four billion dollars a decade ago costs nineteen billion now. "The welfare department is not trying to get smaller," the operator told me, which is the entire political economy in eight words. And what the system rewards, in the end, is not clinical outcomes, which it does not really measure, but jobs and the absence of catastrophe. "What does every politician say? Jobs, jobs, jobs." A provider who employs a hundred people in a legislator's district and never produces a headline is not a vendor. He is a constituent the system needs.

Now widen the lens to Washington, which sits above all of this and pulls in two directions at once. It is the reason the community provider exists at all: federal law, through Olmstead and the Justice Department that enforces it, pushes people out of institutions, and the federal match makes community care cheaper for the state than the institution it replaced. But the same federal government is now withdrawing money on a scale this system has never faced. The 2025 reconciliation law cuts federal Medicaid by roughly nine hundred billion dollars over a decade, by the Congressional Budget Office's reckoning, through work requirements and provider-tax limits and the rest of the machinery.1515CBO, via KFF: about $911B net in federal Medicaid reductions over 2025-2034. People with disabilities are largely shielded from the coverage provisions (work requirements fall on the ACA expansion population, with disability exemptions). Per-capita caps and a cut to the expansion match were debated but did not pass. Treat the figures as CBO projections. People with severe disabilities are largely shielded from the coverage cuts; they are not the target. But money is fungible, and the timing is the part an investor must sit with. The mechanisms begin to bite in 2027, yet roughly three-quarters of the dollars come out in the back half of the decade, between 2030 and 2034.1616KFF's allocation of the CBO score: about 76% of the ten-year reductions land in 2030-2034. The California LAO estimates the law raises net Medi-Cal state costs by roughly $3.2B through 2029-30, against a structural deficit widening toward $22B. A fund buying in 2026 meets the heaviest cuts in its exit window. California, which already funds two-thirds of this system from its own General Fund and faces a widening deficit, will have to backfill, and the only lever it has is the one it always pulls. A fund that buys this cash flow in 2026 absorbs the squeeze precisely as it is trying to sell. The federal overhang is real. It is not a reason to avoid the asset. It is a reason to buy it cheaply, and to buy it at the severe end, where the price is floored by the cost of the alternative rather than by a schedule the state can simply cut.

VI

California Is the Moat

"private nonprofit community agencies shall be utilized by the state"California Welfare & Institutions Code §4620, on the regional centers

There is a lazy version of the bear case that says: it's California, the worst state in the country to do business, the highest taxes and the heaviest regulation, and you want to plant capital there. I want to take that seriously and then turn it over, because the regulation that terrifies generalist capital is, in this specific business, the wall around the moat.

Start with what California is. It is an entitlement, and an entitlement is a demand floor no other state offers. Everywhere else, disability services are rationed by a waiver with a fixed number of slots and a waiting list that runs for years; more than seven hundred thousand Americans are on those lists right now. California has no list. The person is owed the service. That does not protect the price, and I have been careful throughout to keep the two apart: the entitlement protects volume, not margin. But it means the demand under this asset cannot be legislated away or capped, which is more than almost any healthcare cash flow can say.

Now layer on the part that cannot be moved. A California provider in this business holds a stack of credentials that exist only in California and only at a specific address: a facility license from one state agency, a specialty certification from another, and a vendor number issued by each regional center it works with, for each kind of service, none of it transferable.1717The license is issued under Title 22 and does not convey on sale (the buyer files a new application); the specialty certification is issued by DDS under Title 17; the vendor number is issued per entity, per regional center, per service code. The certified administrator is credentialed as an individual. Closing therefore runs six to nine months for regulatory re-approval, against a normal 60 to 90 days. The license does not convey when the business is sold; the buyer applies for a new one. The administrator who holds the home together is certified as an individual, not as a company asset. Closing an acquisition takes not the usual two or three months but six to nine, because the regulators must re-approve the whole stack. You cannot pick this business up and carry it to Texas, and you would not want to: Texas is a waiting-list state with lower rates. The thicket that makes generalist investors run is the same thicket that means perhaps five to ten operators in the entire state can clear the bar to do the severe work at all. Much of what those generalists are actually afraid of, the new California laws policing private equity in healthcare, does not even apply here; those laws were written for physician and dental practices, not for the kind of licensed community home this is.1818SB 351, AB 1415, and the office of health-care affordability review target physician and dental management structures, not Title 22 community-care facilities. Generalist capital mis-prices the asset by applying physician-practice rules to a community home. They are pricing the wrong risk, and their fear is my barrier to entry.

Then comes the strangest and most important structural fact, the one that took me longest to understand. This entire system is built on nonprofits, and that is not an accident of culture; it is written into the statute. The law requires that the regional centers, the twenty-one buyers that sit between the state and every provider, be private nonprofit corporations.1919Welfare & Institutions Code §4620 and §4621: the Legislature found the work "of such a special and unique nature" that the state should not run it directly, and mandated private nonprofit corporations as regional centers. The mandate binds the 21 intermediaries, not the providers beneath them, who may be for-profit or nonprofit. The provider world beneath them grew out of a parent movement that began in the 1950s, out of church groups and family cooperatives, and it is still overwhelmingly nonprofit and small: ten thousand providers nationally, most under five million dollars in revenue, none with real scale. The consensus reads that fragmentation as a roll-up opportunity, and at the low-acuity end it is one. But two things about the nonprofit base change the picture at the severe end. First, you cannot actually buy a nonprofit. Its assets are locked in a charitable trust; converting it requires the state attorney general's consent and a fair-value appraisal; there is no equity to acquire. The buyable universe is only the for-profit slice of a historically nonprofit market.2020A nonprofit public-benefit corporation holds its assets in charitable trust and cannot convert to for-profit without the California Attorney General's written consent and an independent valuation. Private equity has acquired more than a thousand disability and elder-care providers since 2013, but it buys the for-profit operators; it does not, and largely cannot, harvest a nonprofit's enterprise value. Second, and more important, the nonprofit and the mom-and-pop cannot fund the severe end. A purpose-built home for the most dangerous behaviors, with a secure perimeter and the staffing ratios the acuity demands, takes capital that a small nonprofit running on a mission and a thin margin does not have, and the founders who built these little organizations in the 1980s are aging out with no succession. So the severe end is starved of capital and starved of operators at the very moment the demographic wave is cresting. That is the white space. It is not that the market resists consolidation; the low end is consolidating fast. It is that the severe end is structurally unbuilt, and almost no one with both the capital and the competence is building it. The operator I am backing keeps his own real estate and runs for profit precisely so that someone, finally, owns the outcome.

VII

The Mispricing

"Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful."Warren Buffett

Put it together and you arrive at a mispricing hiding in plain sight, of a kind that does not come along often.

On one side, the demand under this asset is a demographic and legal certainty: an aging-caregiver cliff with no institutional backstop, an entitlement that cannot be capped, a years-long national waiting list for the rationed version of the same service. On the other side, the capital that would normally chase a cash flow like that is running the other way as fast as it can. The largest national operator is junk-rated and was pushed out of the severe line. The antitrust regulators just forced the biggest deal in the sector to divest more than a hundred facilities.2121The Federal Trade Commission's January 2026 consent order required Sevita to divest 128 intermediate-care facilities to clear its acquisition of a competitor's residential business. Roll-up at national scale now draws antitrust friction; even the winner had to shrink. The marquee platforms are stuck in holds they cannot exit, and the whole industry is bracing for the federal cuts. The buyer universe for severe-end disability care is thinning at the precise moment the need is surging. That is the setup value investors wait years for: a durable, non-cyclical, legally protected cash flow that the marginal buyer has been trained to flee.

The market is pricing one number and missing the asset behind it. It sees ninety-five percent of revenue from a single government payor and reads a single point of failure. I see a sovereign-backed annuity on a population the state is legally forbidden to abandon, with a years-long line of people waiting to get in, at a price floored not by a schedule the state can cut but by the cost of the jail cell and the institution that are the only alternatives. The concentration the market fears is the durability I am buying.

None of which means the price is whatever the seller wants. The discipline is the whole game, and it is the exact opposite of the playbook that bankrupted CARD. You buy at the low end of the range, where the downside still returns your capital even if rates stagnate for years. You do not load the homes with debt and pull a dividend; the leverage that looks clever in the model is the thing that turns a rate freeze into a bankruptcy. You keep the founder, because in this business the relationships and the license literally walk out the door with the person. And you understand, in a way the cost-cutters never did, that here the moral architecture and the financial architecture are the same structure. The way you destroy this asset is to cut the staffing, which fails the compliance, which loses the license, which empties the home. Quality is not a constraint on the return. At the severe end, quality is the return.

§

Go back to the house. The lawn, the basketball hoop, the man in his forties who will not be going to an institution because there is no institution left to send him to, and who is not in a jail cell because someone was willing to take him. Four people will care for him tonight, and the state will pay for it, because every other option costs more and most of them are illegal. The returns on this are real, and I intend to earn them. But the reason the moat is durable is not financial engineering. It is that the work is genuinely hard, and genuinely good, and almost no one with capital is willing to do it well.

That is the whole thesis, and it has been hiding in the part of the system everyone was trained to look away from. Trust the trendline. The fear is the moat. Severity is the moat.
